The U.S. government's recent crackdown on Anthropic, primarily occurring in 2026, stemmed from a multi-faceted dispute concerning national security, control over advanced AI capabilities, and Anthropic's policy on technology use [2][7][15][16][18][27].

## Reasons for the Crackdown

### Refusal to Waive "Red Lines"
A primary catalyst for the government's action was Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei's refusal to comply with demands from Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth [2][10]. Hegseth pressured Amodei to remove "red lines" that restricted the use of Anthropic's AI model, Claude, for **mass domestic surveillance** and **fully autonomous weapons systems** [2][16]. Anthropic had maintained these safeguards since signing a contract with the Pentagon in 2025, specifically to prevent its technology from being used for mass surveillance [10].

### Designation as a National Security Supply-Chain Risk
Following Amodei's refusal, the Trump Administration, in an unprecedented move against an American company, designated Anthropic as a **"Supply-Chain Risk to National Security"** [2][15][25][28][31][34][38][39][42][48][54][57]. On February 27, 2026, President Trump directed all federal agencies to cease using Anthropic's technology [15][25][28][31][34][39][42][54][57]. The government's justification for this designation included concerns that Anthropic could potentially **disable or alter its technology to serve its own interests** rather than U.S. national priorities during wartime, questioning its reliability as a "trusted partner" [33][35][40][41][51][52]. The dispute, from the government's perspective, reportedly arose from Anthropic's behavior during contract negotiations, not merely the limitations it set on its technology [33][40][41][51][52].

### Export Controls on Advanced AI Models
In June 2026, the U.S. Department of Commerce issued an **export control directive**, ordering Anthropic to suspend access to its newly released advanced AI models, **Fable 5 and Mythos 5**, for all foreign nationals [18][27][30][36][37][43][44][45][46][49][53][55][56][58]. Anthropic consequently disabled these models for all users globally to comply with the order [18][30][45][55][56]. The government's concern centered on the belief that a method existed to "jailbreak" or bypass Fable 5's safeguards, allowing it to be exploited for **identifying software vulnerabilities** [18][30][36][44][45][55][58]. Anthropic, while complying, stated it received little detailed explanation for the order and noted that similar capabilities were available from other publicly deployed models [18][44][58].

### Alleged Role of Amazon
**[Uncertain]** Some reports suggest that the crackdown on Anthropic's advanced AI models was prompted by discussions between the Trump administration and Amazon CEO Andy Jassy [23][32]. Reportedly, researchers at Amazon, an investor and cloud partner of Anthropic, found an exploit in the Mythos architecture and reported it directly to the government, potentially providing the technical pretext for the immediate shutdown [24][47].

## Analysis
The U.S. government's actions against Anthropic represent a significant shift in AI policy, moving beyond hardware restrictions to direct control over deployed AI models, particularly in the context of national security [37][46][53]. While the core conflict stems from Anthropic's principled stance against certain military and surveillance applications of its AI, the government framed its actions around broader national security concerns regarding the company's reliability and the potential misuse of its technology [2][33][35][40][41][51][52].

The crackdown has drawn criticism from trade groups, former defense officials, and academics, who warned that designating an American tech company as a national security risk could have a **chilling effect on U.S. innovation** and weaken the country's competitiveness in the AI sector [15][25][28][29][31][34][38][39][42][48][54][57]. Microsoft also supported Anthropic, arguing against putting the U.S. AI ecosystem at risk [15][25][28][31][34][39][42][54][57].

Despite the initial ban, Anthropic filed lawsuits challenging the Department of Defense's designation [17][33][35][40][41][51][52]. A federal judge granted a preliminary injunction against the ban, stating it was "likely both contrary to law and arbitrary and capricious," complicating the Pentagon's efforts [60]. There have also been signs of easing tensions, with the White House reportedly drafting plans to permit federal agencies to use Anthropic's tools [16].

## Caveats
The precise sequence and full scope of events leading to the crackdown are still unfolding, particularly regarding the specific technical vulnerabilities cited for the export controls and the alleged involvement of other corporations like Amazon [18][23][24][30][32][44][47][58]. The government's justification for the "supply-chain risk" designation also includes a broader concern about Anthropic's long-term alignment with national interests, beyond just the stated "red lines" [33][35][40][41][51][52].
